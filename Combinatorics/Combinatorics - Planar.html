Planar Graphs

Euler's Theorem​

Definition

Let GGG be a graph that can be drawn on a plane surface so that no two of its edges intersect. Then GGG is called a planar graph.

Euler's Theorem on Planar Graphs​

Let GGG be a connected planar graph with VVV vertices, EEE edges, and FFF faces. Then V+F=E+2V+F=E+2V+F=E+2.

Proof​

We prove the statement by induction on EEE, the number of edges of GGG. If E=1E=1E=1, then GGG is either the tree of one edge, and then V=2,F=1V=2, F=1V=2,F=1, and the statement is true, or GGG is the one-vertex graph with a loop, and then V=1,F=2V=1, F=2V=1,F=2, and the statement is true again.

Now let us assume that we know the statement for all graphs with E−1E-1E−1 edges, and let GGG have EEE edges. We distinguish two cases.

If we can remove an edge eee from GGG so that the new graph G′G^{\prime}G′ is still connected, then eee is in a cycle in GGG, and therefore there are two different faces on the two sides of eee in GGG. Then G′G^{\prime}G′ has E−1E-1E−1 edges, VVV vertices, and F−1F-1F−1 faces as the removal of eee turned the two faces on the two sides of eee into one. Therefore, V+F−1=E−1+2V+F-1=E-1+2V+F−1=E−1+2, so V+F=E+2V+F=E+2V+F=E+2.

If there is no eee with the mentioned property, then GGG is a cycle-free connected graph, that is, a tree. Then we know that V=E+1V=E+1V=E+1. On the other hand, F=1F=1F=1, so the claim is again true.

Lemma 1​

The graph K3,3K_{3, 3}K3,3​ is not planar.

Let us suppose that K3,3K_{3, 3}K3,3​ is planar. As it has nine edges and six vertices, and it must have five faces. However, K3,3K_{3, 3}K3,3​ is a complete bipartite graph, so all its faces must be quadrilaterals. Five quadrilaterals have a total of twenty edges, but in a planar graph, each edge is contained in two faces. Therefore, our graph would need ten distinct edges, but it has only nine.

Lemma 2​

The graph K5K_5K5​ is not planar.

Again, let us suppose that K5K_5K5​ is planar. As it has five vertices and ten edges, it follows from Euler's Theorem that it must have seven faces. As K5K_5K5​ is a complete graph, all its faces must be triangles. Seven triangles, however, would need 21 edges, which is impossible as each of the ten edges of K5K_5K5​ is used in exactly two faces.

Kuratowski's Theorem​

A graph is not planar if and only if it contains a subgraph that is edge-equivalent to K5K_5K5​ or K3,3K_{3, 3}K3,3​.

Let PPP be a convex polyhedron with VVV vertices, FFF faces, and EEE edges. Then V+F=E+2V + F = E + 2V+F=E+2.

All convex polyhedra have at least one face that has at most five edges.

Coloring Maps​

Proposition​

The vertices of any planar graph can be properly colored with six colors.

Proof​

Induction on VVV, the number of vertices of the planar graph GGG. If V=1V=1V=1, then the statement is obviously true. Let us assume that we know that the statement is true for graphs with V−1V-1V−1 vertices. Let GGG have VVV vertices. Then we know that GGG has a vertex AAA of degree at most five. Remove AAA from GGG to get the graph G′G^{\prime}G′. By our induction hypothesis, G′G^{\prime}G′ has a proper coloring with six colors. Take such a coloring of G′G^{\prime}G′, then color AAA with a color that is not the color of any of its (at most five) neighbors.

This means, by duality, that all maps can be properly colored using six colors. The situation is significantly harder if we only want to use five colors. The result, however, is the same.

Theorem​

The vertices of any planar graph can be properly colored with five colors.

Proof​

Just as in proving the previous proposition, we use induction. The only case in which the previous proof does not work is when AAA has five neighbors, and they are all of different colors. In this case, denote by 1,2,3,41,2,3,41,2,3,4 and 5 the colors of the five neighbors y1,y2,y3,y4,y5y_1, y_2, y_3, y_4, y_5y1​,y2​,y3​,y4​,y5​ of AAA as they follow clockwise. Let G′G^{\prime}G′ be the graph obtained from GGG by removing AAA and all the edges adjacent to AAA. If G′G^{\prime}G′ has a proper 5-coloring in which y1y_1y1​ and y3y_3y3​ are the same color, then we are done. If not, then any proper 5-coloring of G′G^{\prime}G′ must contain a path from y1y_1y1​ to y3y_3y3​ along which the vertices are alternatingly colored 1 and 3 . By a similar argument, if y2y_2y2​ and y4y_4y4​ cannot be the same color, then any proper 5 -coloring of G′G^{\prime}G′ must contain a path from y2y_2y2​ to y4y_4y4​ along which the vertices are alternatingly colored 2 and 4. This, however, is a contradiction, as a path from y1y_1y1​ to y3y_3y3​ and a path from y2y_2y2​ to y4y_4y4​ must always intersect.
