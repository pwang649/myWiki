Ramsey Theory

Ramsey Theory for Finite Graphs​

Six people are waiting in the lobby of a hotel. Prove that there are either three of them who know each other, or three of them who do not know each other.

Ramsey theorem for graphs​

Let kkk and ℓ\ellℓ be two positive integers, both of which are at least two. Then there exists a (minimal) positive integer R(k,ℓ)R(k, \ell)R(k,ℓ) so that if we color the edges of a complete graph with R(k,ℓ)R(k, \ell)R(k,ℓ) vertices red and blue, then this graph will either have a KkK_kKk​ subgraph with only red edges, or a KℓK_{\ell}Kℓ​ subgraph with only blue edges.

Proof​

We prove the statement by a new version of mathematical induction on kkk and ℓ\ellℓ. This induction will run as follows. First we prove the initial conditions that R(k,2)R(k, 2)R(k,2) and R(2,ℓ)R(2, \ell)R(2,ℓ) exist for all kkk, and all ℓ\ellℓ. Then we take the induction step, proving that if R(k,ℓ−1)R(k, \ell-1)R(k,ℓ−1) exists, and also R(k−1,ℓ)R(k-1, \ell)R(k−1,ℓ) exists, then R(k,ℓ)R(k, \ell)R(k,ℓ) also exists.

To see that the initial conditions hold, note that R(k,2)=kR(k, 2)=kR(k,2)=k, and similarly, R(2,ℓ)=ℓR(2, \ell)=\ellR(2,ℓ)=ℓ. Indeed, either all edges of a KkK_kKk​ are red, and then it has a KkK_kKk​ subgraph with all edges red, or at least one of its edges is blue, in which case it has a K2K_2K2​ subgraph with all edges blue. Analogous argument works for R(2,ℓ)R(2, \ell)R(2,ℓ) We prove the induction step by showing that

R(k,ℓ)≤R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)R(k, \ell) \leq R(k, \ell-1)+R(k-1, \ell)R(k,ℓ)≤R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)

Indeed, take a complete graph with R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)R(k, \ell-1)+R(k-1, \ell)R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ) vertices. Take one of its vertices, and call it VVV. As VVV has degree R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)−1R(k, \ell-1)+R(k-1, \ell)-1R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)−1, it has either at least R(k,ℓ−1)R(k, \ell-1)R(k,ℓ−1) blue edges adjacent to it, or it has at least R(k−1,ℓ)R(k-1, \ell)R(k−1,ℓ) red edges adjacent to it.

In the first case, let bbb denote the R(k,ℓ−1)R(k, \ell-1)R(k,ℓ−1)-element set of the other endpoints of these blue edges. Then, by the definition of R(k,ℓ−1)R(k, \ell-1)R(k,ℓ−1), the set bbb either contains a monochromatic red KkK_kKk​ and we are done, or a monochromatic blue Kℓ−1K_{\ell-1}Kℓ−1​, which can be completed to a monochromatic blue KℓK \ellKℓ by adding the vertex VVV, and we are done.

In the second case, let rrr denote the R(k−1,ℓ)R(k-1, \ell)R(k−1,ℓ)-element set of the other endpoints of these red edges. Then again, rrr either contains a monochromatic blue KℓK_{\ell}Kℓ​ and we are done, or a monochromatic red Kk−1K_{k-1}Kk−1​, which can be completed to a monochromatic red KkK_kKk​ by adding the vertex VVV, and we are done again.

So the inequality is proved, therefore the induction step is proved, and therefore the theorem is proved.

Corollary​

For all positive integers k≥2k \geq 2k≥2 and ℓ≥2\ell \geq 2ℓ≥2, the inequality

R(k,ℓ)≤R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)R(k, \ell) \leq R(k, \ell-1)+R(k-1, \ell)R(k,ℓ)≤R(k,ℓ−1)+R(k−1,ℓ)

holds.

Generalizations of the Ramsey Theorem​

A circle of 17 friends has the property that no matter how we choose two from these 17 friends, those two people correspond with each other on one of three given subjects. Prove that there are three friends among the circle of these 17 friends such that any two of the three of them correspond with each other on the same subject.

The Erdős-Szekeres theorem​

Let nnn be a positive integer. Then there exists a (minimal) positive integer ES(n)E S(n)ES(n) so that if there are N≥ES(n)N \geq E S(n)N≥ES(n) points given in the plane, no three of which are collinear, then we can choose nnn points from them that form a convex nnn-gon.

Proof​

We claim that R3(n,n)R_3(n, n)R3​(n,n) will always be such a positive integer (not necessarily the minimal one). Take the complete graph whose vertices are our R3(n,n)R_3(n, n)R3​(n,n) points in the plane. Color its triangles red or blue according to the following rule. Number the points from 1 to R3(n,n)R_3(n, n)R3​(n,n), and color a triangle red if the path from the smallest number via the middle one to the largest one is clockwise. Color a triangle blue if that path is counterclockwise.

As our graph has R3(n,n)R_3(n, n)R3​(n,n) vertices, there will be a KnK_nKn​ subgraph with monochromatic triangles. We claim that the vertices of this KnK_nKn​ subgraph form a convex nnn-gon. To see this, it suffices to show that there are no four vertices in this subgraph so that one is within the triangle spanned by the other three. Let us assume without loss of generality that A<B<CA<B<CA<B<C, and that all triangles of our K4K_4K4​ at hand are red. Then the fact that triangle ADBA D BADB is red forces D<A<BD<A<BD<A<B.
