The Notion of Probability​

Definition

Let Ω\OmegaΩ be a finite set of outcomes of some sequence of trials, so that all these outcomes are equally likely. Let A⊆ΩA \subseteq \OmegaA⊆Ω. Then Ω\OmegaΩ is called a sample space, and AAA is called an event. The ratio

P(A)=∣A∣∣Ω∣P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|\Omega|}P(A)=∣Ω∣∣A∣​

P(A1∪A2∪⋯∪An)≤P(A1)+P(A2)+⋯+P(An).P\left(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n\right) \leq P\left(A_1\right)+P\left(A_2\right)+\cdots+P\left(A_n\right) .P(A1​∪A2​∪⋯∪An​)≤P(A1​)+P(A2​)+⋯+P(An​).

We simply have to show that

∣A1∪⋯∪An∣≤∣A1∣+⋯+∣An∣.\left|A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n\right| \leq\left|A_1\right|+\cdots+\left|A_n\right| .∣A1​∪⋯∪An​∣≤∣A1​∣+⋯+∣An​∣.

Conditional Probability​

Definition

If AAA and BBB are two events from the same sample space Ω\OmegaΩ, and P(A∩B)=P(A)⋅P(B)P(A \cap B)=P(A) \cdot P(B)P(A∩B)=P(A)⋅P(B), then AAA and BBB are called independent events. Otherwise they are called dependent.

Definition

Let AAA and BBB be events from the same sample space, and assume P(B)>0P(B)>0P(B)>0. Let

P(A∣B)=P(A∩B)P(B)P(A \mid B)=\frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}P(A∣B)=P(B)P(A∩B)​

Then P(A∣B)P(A \mid B)P(A∣B) is called a conditional probability, and is read "the probability of AAA given BBB ".

That is, P(A∣B)P(A \mid B)P(A∣B) is the probability of AAA given that BBB occurs. The following proposition is now immediate from the definitions.

Proposition​

The events AAA and BBB are independent if and only if P(A∣B)=P(A)P(A \mid B)=P(A)P(A∣B)=P(A) holds

Bayes' Theorem​

Let AAA and BBB be mutually exclusive events so that A∪B=ΩA \cup B = \OmegaA∪B=Ω. Let CCC be any event. Then

P(C)=P(C∣A)⋅P(A)+P(C∣B)⋅P(B).P(C) = P(C \mid A) \cdot P(A) + P(C \mid B) \cdot P(B).P(C)=P(C∣A)⋅P(A)+P(C∣B)⋅P(B).

Proof​

As AAA and BBB are mutually exclusive, A∩CA \cap CA∩C and B∩CB \cap CB∩C are disjoint, and since A∪B=ΩA \cup B = \OmegaA∪B=Ω, their union is exactly CCC. Therefore,

P(C)=P(C∩A)+P(C∩B),P(C) = P(C \cap A) + P(C \cap B),P(C)=P(C∩A)+P(C∩B),

and the proof follows as the first (resp. second) member of the right-hand side agrees with the first (resp. second) member of the right-hand side of the Bayes' Theorem.
