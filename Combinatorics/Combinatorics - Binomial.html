The Binomial Theorem​

Theorem I​

For all nonnegative integers nnn,

(x+y)n=∑k=0n(nk)xkyn−k.(x+y)^n=\sum_{k=0}^n\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right) x^k y^{n-k} .(x+y)n=k=0∑n​(nk​)xkyn−k.

Proof​

Consider the product of nnn sums, (x+y)(x+y)⋯(x+y)(x+y)(x+y) \cdots(x+y)(x+y)(x+y)⋯(x+y). When computing this product, we take one summand from each parentheses, multiply them together, then repeat this in all of 2n2^n2n possible ways and add the results. We get a product equal to xkyn−kx^k y^{n-k}xkyn−k each time we take kkk summands equal to xxx. There are (nk)k\left(\begin{array}{l}n \\ k\end{array}\right) k(nk​)k-element subsets of the set of all nnn parentheses, so we will get such a term (nk)\left(\begin{array}{l}n \\ k\end{array}\right)(nk​) times, and the proof is complete.

Theorem II​

For all positive integers nnn, the alternating sum of binomial coefficients (nk)\left(\begin{array}{l}n \\ k\end{array}\right)(nk​) is zero. In other words,

∑k=0n(−1)k⋅(nk)=0\sum_{k=0}^n(-1)^k \cdot\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right)=0k=0∑n​(−1)k⋅(nk​)=0

Proof​

Applying the binomial theorem with x=−1x=-1x=−1 and y=1y=1y=1 we immediately get our claim.

Theorem III​

For all nonnegative integers nnn and kkk, the identity

(nk)+(nk+1)=(n+1k+1)\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{c} n \\ k+1 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} n+1 \\ k+1 \end{array}\right)(nk​)+(nk+1​)=(n+1k+1​)

holds.

Proof​

The right-hand side is, by definition, the number of k+1k+1k+1-element subsets of [n+1][n+1][n+1]. Such a subset SSS either contains the element n+1n+1n+1, or it does not. If it does, then the rest of SSS is a kkk-element subset of [n][n][n], and these are enumerated by the first member of the left-hand side. If it does not, then SSS is a k+1k+1k+1-element subset of [n][n][n], and these are enumerated by the second member of the left-hand side.

Theorem IV​

For all nonnegative integers n,

2n=∑k=0n(nk)2^n=\sum_{k=0}^n\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right)2n=k=0∑n​(nk​)

Proof I​

Both sides count the number of all subsets of an nnn-element set. The left-hand side counts directly, while the right-hand side counts the number of kkk-element subsets, then sums over kkk.

Proof II​

Apply the binomial theorem with x=y=1x=y=1x=y=1.

Theorem V​

For all nonnegative integers kkk and nnn,

(kk)+(k+1k)+(k+2k)+⋯+(nk)=(n+1k+1).\left(\begin{array}{l} k \\ k \end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{c} k+1 \\ k \end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{c} k+2 \\ k \end{array}\right)+\cdots+\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} n+1 \\ k+1 \end{array}\right) .(kk​)+(k+1k​)+(k+2k​)+⋯+(nk​)=(n+1k+1​).

Proof​

The right-hand side clearly counts all k+1k+1k+1-element subsets of [n+1][n+1][n+1]. The left-hand side counts the same, separated into cases according to the largest element. That is, there are (kk)\left(\begin{array}{l}k \\ k\end{array}\right)(kk​) subsets of [n+1][n+1][n+1] that have k+1k+1k+1 elements whose largest element is k+1k+1k+1; there are (k+1k)\left(\begin{array}{c}k+1 \\ k\end{array}\right)(k+1k​) subsets of [n+1][n+1][n+1] that have k+1k+1k+1 elements whose largest element is k+2k+2k+2, and so on. In general, there are (k+ik)\left(\begin{array}{c}k+i \\ k\end{array}\right)(k+ik​) subsets of [n+1][n+1][n+1] that have k+1k+1k+1 elements whose largest element is k+i+1k+i+1k+i+1, for all i≤n−ki \leq n-ki≤n−k. Indeed, if the largest element of such a subset is k+i+1k+i+1k+i+1, then its remaining kkk elements must form a subset of [k+i][k+i][k+i].

Theorem VI​

For all nonnegative integers nnn, the identity

∑k=1nk(nk)=n2n−1\sum_{k=1}^n k\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right)=n 2^{n-1}k=1∑n​k(nk​)=n2n−1

holds. Before proving the theorem, note that it is not even obvious why

∑k=1nk(nk)2n−1\frac{\sum_{k=1}^n k\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right)}{2^{n-1}}2n−1∑k=1n​k(nk​)​

should be an integer. Our proof will show that it is not only an integer, it is equal to nnn. This hopefully convinces the reader that binomial coefficient identities are beautiful.

Proof I​

Both sides count the number of ways to choose a committee among nnn people, then to choose a president from the committee. On the left-hand side, we first choose a kkk-member committee in (nk)\left(\begin{array}{l}n \\ k\end{array}\right)(nk​) ways, then we choose its president in kkk ways. On the right-hand side, we first choose the president in nnn ways, then we choose a subset of the remaining (n−1)(n-1)(n−1)-member set of people for the role of non-president committee members in 2n−12^{n-1}2n−1 ways.

We provide another proof that uses the binomial theorem. It also gives us an early hint that sometimes very finite-looking problems, such as choice problems, can be solved by using methods from infinite calculus, such as functions and their derivatives.

Proof II​

Apply the binomial theorem with y=1y=1y=1 to get the identity

(x+1)n=∑k=0n(nk)xk.(x+1)^n=\sum_{k=0}^n\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right) x^k .(x+1)n=k=0∑n​(nk​)xk.

Both sides are differentiable functions of the variable xxx. So we can take their derivatives with respect to xxx, and they must be equal. This yields

n(x+1)n−1=∑k=1nk⋅(nk)xk−1.n(x+1)^{n-1}=\sum_{k=1}^n k \cdot\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right) x^{k-1} .n(x+1)n−1=k=1∑n​k⋅(nk​)xk−1.

Now substitute x=1x=1x=1.

Theorem VII (Vandermonde's identity)​

For all positive integers nnn, mmm, and kkk,

(n+mk)=∑i=0k(ni)(mk−i).\left(\begin{array}{c} n+m \\ k \end{array}\right)=\sum_{i=0}^k\left(\begin{array}{c} n \\ i \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} m \\ k-i \end{array}\right) .(n+mk​)=i=0∑k​(ni​)(mk−i​).

Proof​

The left-hand side counts all kkk-element subsets of [n+m][n+m][n+m]. The right-hand side counts the same, according to the number of elements chosen from [n][n][n]. Indeed, we can first choose iii elements from [n][n][n] in (ni)\left(\begin{array}{l}n \\ i\end{array}\right)(ni​) ways, then choose the remaining k−ik-ik−i elements from the set {n+1,n+2,⋯ ,n+m}\{n+1, n+2, \cdots, n+m\}{n+1,n+2,⋯,n+m} in (mk−i)\left(\begin{array}{c}m \\ k-i\end{array}\right)(mk−i​) ways.

The Multinomial Theorem​

Definition​

Let n=∑i=1kain=\sum_{i=1}^k a_in=∑i=1k​ai​, where nnn and a1,a2,⋯ ,aka_1, a_2, \cdots, a_ka1​,a2​,⋯,ak​ are nonnegative integers. We define

(na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)=n!a1!⋅a2!⋯ak!.\left(\begin{array}{c} n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k \end{array}\right)=\frac{n !}{a_{1} ! \cdot a_{2} ! \cdots a_{k} !} .(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​)=a1​!⋅a2​!⋯ak​!n!​.

The numbers (na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(\begin{array}{c}n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\end{array}\right)(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​) are called multinomial coefficients.

Theorem I (Multinomial theorem)​

For all nonnegative integers nnn and kkk, the equality

(x1+x2+⋯+xk)n=∑a1,a2,⋯ ,ak(na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)x1a1x2a2⋯xkak\left(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k\right)^n=\sum_{a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k}\left(\begin{array}{c} n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k \end{array}\right) x_1^{a_1} x_2^{a_2} \cdots x_k^{a_k}(x1​+x2​+⋯+xk​)n=a1​,a2​,⋯,ak​∑​(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​)x1a1​​x2a2​​⋯xkak​​

holds. Here the sum is taken over all kkk-tuples of nonnegative integers a1,a2,⋯ ,aka_1, a_2, \cdots, a_ka1​,a2​,⋯,ak​ such that n=∑i=1kain=\sum_{i=1}^k a_in=∑i=1k​ai​

Proof​

We have to show that the term x1a1x2a2⋯xkakx_1^{a_1} x_2^{a_2} \cdots x_k^{a_k}x1a1​​x2a2​​⋯xkak​​ can be obtained in exactly (na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(\begin{array}{c}n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\end{array}\right)(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​) ways as a product of kkk variables, one from each parentheses of (x1+x2+⋯+xk)⋯(x1+x2+⋯+xk)\left(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k\right) \cdots\left(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k\right)(x1​+x2​+⋯+xk​)⋯(x1​+x2​+⋯+xk​). To obtain such a term, we have to choose xix_ixi​ from exactly iii parentheses, for all i∈[k]i \in[k]i∈[k].

Now let us take aia_iai​ copies of xix_ixi​, for all i∈[k]i \in[k]i∈[k], and order these nnn letters linearly. Theorem 3.53.53.5 shows that this can be done in exactly (na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(\begin{array}{c}n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\end{array}\right)(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​) ways. On the other hand, each linear ordering ppp defines a natural way of choosing variables from the parentheses. Indeed, if the jjj th letter of ppp is xix_ixi​, then from the jjj th parentheses, we choose xix_ixi​. This way our (na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(\begin{array}{c}n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\end{array}\right)(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​) linear orderings will produce exactly (na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(\begin{array}{c}n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\end{array}\right)(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​) terms that are equal to x1a1x2a2⋯xkakx_1^{a_1} x_2^{a_2} \cdots x_k^{a_k}x1a1​​x2a2​​⋯xkak​​.

It is clear that this procedure establishes a bijection from the set of linear orderings of nnn letters, aia_iai​ of which is equal to xix_ixi​ for all i∈[k]i \in[k]i∈[k] onto that of terms of (x1+x2+⋯+xk)n\left(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k\right)^n(x1​+x2​+⋯+xk​)n that are equal to x1a1x2a2⋯xkakx_1^{a_1} x_2^{a_2} \cdots x_k^{a_k}x1a1​​x2a2​​⋯xkak​​. Therefore, the coefficient of x1a1x2a2⋯xkakx_1^{a_1} x_2^{a_2} \cdots x_k^{a_k}x1a1​​x2a2​​⋯xkak​​ in (x1+x2+⋯+xk)n\left(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k\right)^n(x1​+x2​+⋯+xk​)n is precisely (na1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(\begin{array}{c}n \\ a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\end{array}\right)(na1​,a2​,⋯,ak​​), and the proof follows.
