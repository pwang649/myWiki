Compositions​

Definition

A sequence (a1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\right)(a1​,a2​,⋯,ak​) of integers fulfilling ai≥0a_i \geq 0ai​≥0 for all iii, and (a1+a2+⋯+ak)=n\left(a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_k\right)=n(a1​+a2​+⋯+ak​)=n is called a weak composition of nnn. If, in addition, the aia_iai​ are positive for all i∈[k]i \in[k]i∈[k], then the sequence (a1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\right)(a1​,a2​,⋯,ak​) is called a composition of nnn.

Theorem​

For all positive integers nnn and kkk, the number of weak compositions of nnn into kkk parts is

(n+k−1k−1)=(n+k−1n).\left(\begin{array}{c} n+k-1 \\ k-1 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{c} n+k-1 \\ n \end{array}\right) .(n+k−1k−1​)=(n+k−1n​).

Proof​

The problem is certainly equivalent to counting the number of ways of putting nnn identical balls into kkk different boxes. Place the kkk boxes in a line, then place the balls in them in some way and align them in the middle of the boxes. This creates a long line consisting of nnn balls and k−1k-1k−1 walls separating the kkk boxes from each other. Note that simply knowing in which order the nnn identical balls and k−1k-1k−1 separating walls follow each other is the same as knowing the number of balls in each box. So our task is reduced to finding the number of ways to permute the multiset consisting of nnn balls and k−1k-1k−1 walls. This number is

(n+k−1)!n!⋅(k−1)!\frac{(n+k-1) !}{n ! \cdot(k-1) !}n!⋅(k−1)!(n+k−1)!​

Corollary 1​

For all positive integers nnn and kkk, the number of compositions of nnn into kkk parts is (n−1k−1)\left(\begin{array}{c}n-1 \\ k-1\end{array}\right)(n−1k−1​).

Proof​

What if a grandparent insists on giving at least one ball to each child? The problem is not any harder. First we can give one ball to each child, then give away the remaining 16 balls to the four children in any of (16+4−14−1)=\left(\begin{array}{c}16+4-1 \\ 4-1\end{array}\right)=(16+4−14−1​)= (193)\left(\begin{array}{c}19 \\ 3\end{array}\right)(193​) ways. The generalization of this argument to nnn balls and kkk children is the following statement.

Corollary 2​

For all positive integers n, the number of all compositions of nnn is 2n−12^{n-1}2n−1.

Proof 1​

A composition of nnn will have at least one and at most nnn parts. So the total number of compositions of nnn is

∑k=1n(n−1k−1)=2n−1\sum_{k=1}^n\left(\begin{array}{l} n-1 \\ k-1 \end{array}\right)=2^{n-1}k=1∑n​(n−1k−1​)=2n−1

Indeed, the left-hand side is the number of all subsets of [n−1][n-1][n−1], first enumerated by their size kkk, and then summed over k∈[n]k \in[n]k∈[n].

Proof 2​

We prove the statement by induction on nnn. For n=1n=1n=1, the statement is true as the integer 1 has one composition. Now assume that the statement is true for nnn, and take all 2n−12^{n-1}2n−1 compositions of nnn. For each such composition CCC, we will define two different compositions of n+1n+1n+1. First, add one to the first element of CCC. This way we get a composition of n+1n+1n+1 with the first element at least 2. Second, take CCC, and write an additional 1 to its front. This way we get a composition of n+1n+1n+1 with first element 1 . It is clear that different compositions of nnn lead to different compositions of n+1n+1n+1 this way. Each decomposition of n+1n+1n+1 can be obtained in exactly one of these two ways. Therefore, it follows that n+1n+1n+1 has twice as many compositions as nnn, which was to be proved.

Set Partitions​

Definition

A partition of the set [n][n][n] is a collection of non-empty blocks so that each element of [n][n][n] belongs to exactly one of these blocks.

The number of partitions of [n][n][n] into kkk nonempty blocks is denoted by S(n,k)S(n, k)S(n,k). The numbers S(n,k)S(n, k)S(n,k) are called the Stirling numbers of the second kind.

Theorem​

For all positive integers k≤nk \leq nk≤n,

S(n,k)=S(n−1,k−1)+k⋅S(n−1,k).S(n, k)=S(n-1, k-1)+k \cdot S(n-1, k) .S(n,k)=S(n−1,k−1)+k⋅S(n−1,k).

Proof​

As before, we can obtain a combinatorial proof by taking a close look at one particular element, say the maximum element nnn. If this element forms a singleton block, then the remaining n−1n-1n−1 elements have exactly S(n−1,k−1)S(n-1, k-1)S(n−1,k−1) ways to complete the partition. These partitions are enumerated by the first member of the right-hand side. If, on the other hand, the element nnn does not form a block by itself, then the remaining n−1n-1n−1 elements must form a partition with kkk blocks in one of S(n−1,k)S(n-1, k)S(n−1,k) ways. Then we can add nnn into any of the kkk blocks formed by this partition, multiplying the number of all our possibilities by kkk. These partitions are enumerated by the second member of the right-hand side. As the left-hand side enumerates all partitions of [n][n][n] into kkk blocks, the claim is proved.

Corollary 1​

The number of all surjective functions f:[n]→[k]f:[n] \rightarrow[k]f:[n]→[k] is k!⋅S(n,k)k ! \cdot S(n, k)k!⋅S(n,k).

Proof​

Such a function defines a partition of [n][n][n]. The blocks are the subsets of elements that are mapped into the same element i∈[k]i \in[k]i∈[k]. Therefore, the blocks are labeled, and there are exactly kkk of them, so the proof follows from the previous paragraph.

An interesting consequence of this is the following unexpected corollary. It is surprising as it shows that xnx^nxn, this very compact expression, is in fact a sum of n+1n+1n+1 terms involving Stirling numbers.

xn=∑k=0nS(n,k)(x)k.x^n=\sum_{k=0}^n S(n, k)(x)_k .xn=k=0∑n​S(n,k)(x)k​.

Proof​

Both sides are polynomials of xxx of degree nnn. So if we can show that they agree for more than nnn values of xxx, we will be done. We will prove an even stronger statement, namely that the two sides agree for all positive integers xxx.

So let xxx be a positive integer. Then the left-hand side is the number of all functions from [n][n][n] to [x][x][x]. We claim that the right-hand side is the same, enumerated according to the size of the image. Indeed, if the image of such a function is of size kkk, then there are (xk)\left(\begin{array}{l}x \\ k\end{array}\right)(xk​) choices for the image III, then, by Corollary 1, there are k!⋅S(n,k)k ! \cdot S(n, k)k!⋅S(n,k) choices for the function itself. As (x)k=k!⋅(xk)(x)_k=k ! \cdot\left(\begin{array}{l}x \\ k\end{array}\right)(x)k​=k!⋅(xk​), the claim is proved.

Integer Partitions​

Definition

Let a1≥a2≥⋯≥ak≥1a_1 \geq a_2 \geq \cdots \geq a_k \geq 1a1​≥a2​≥⋯≥ak​≥1 be integers so that a1+a_1+a1​+ a2+⋯+ak=na_2+\cdots+a_k=na2​+⋯+ak​=n. Then the sequence (a1,a2,⋯ ,ak)\left(a_1, a_2, \cdots, a_k\right)(a1​,a2​,⋯,ak​) is called a partition of the integer nnn. The number of all partitions of nnn is denoted by p(n)p(n)p(n). The number of partitions of nnn into exactly kkk parts is denoted by pk(n)p_k(n)pk​(n).

Definition

A partition of n is called self-conjugate if it is equal to its conjugate.

Theorem​

The number of partitions of nnn into at most kkk parts is equal to that of partitions of nnn into parts not larger than kkk.

Proof​

The first number is equal to that of Ferrers shapes of size nnn with at most kkk rows. The second number is equal to that of Ferrers shapes with at most kkk columns. Finally, these two sets of Ferrers shapes are equinumerous as one can see by taking conjugates.

Theorem​

The number of partitions of nnn into distinct odd parts is equal to that of all self-conjugate partitions of nnn.

Proof​

We define a bijection fff from the set of self-conjugate partitions of nnn onto that of partitions of nnn into distinct odd parts as follows.
