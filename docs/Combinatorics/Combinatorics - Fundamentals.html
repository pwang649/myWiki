Fundamentals

Theorem​

Let nnn and kkk be positive integers, and let n>kn>kn>k. Suppose we have to place nnn identical balls into kkk identical boxes. Then there will be at least one box in which we place at least two balls.

Proof by Contradiction​

Let us assume there is no box with at least two balls. Then each of the kkk boxes has either 0 or 1 ball in it. Denote by mmm the number of boxes that have zero balls in them; then certainly m≥0m \geq 0m≥0. Then, of course, there are k−mk-mk−m boxes that have one. However, that would mean that the total number of balls placed into the kkk boxes is k−mk-mk−m which is a contradiction because we had to place nnn balls into the boxes, and k−m≤k<nk−m \leq k < nk−m≤k<n. Therefore, our assumption that there is no box with at least two balls must have been false.

Theorem (Generalized Pigeon-hole Principle)​

Let n,mn, mn,m, and rrr be positive integers so that n>rmn>r mn>rm, and let us distribute nnn identical balls into mmm identical boxes. Then there will be at least one box into which we place at least r+1r+1r+1 balls.

Proof​

Just as before, we assume the contrary statement. Then each of the mmm boxes can hold at most rrr balls, so all the boxes can hold at most rm<nr m<nrm<n balls, which contradicts the requirement that we distribute nnn balls.

Components of Inductive Proof​

Inductive proof is composed of 3 major parts : Base Case, Induction Hypothesis, Inductive Step.

  1. Base Case: One or more particular cases that represent the most basic case. (e.g. n=1n=1n=1 to prove a statement in the range of positive integer)
  2. Induction Hypothesis: Assumption that we would like to be based on. (e.g. Let's assume that P(k)\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{k})P(k) holds)
  3. Inductive Step: Prove the next step based on the induction hypothesis. (i.e. Show that Induction hypothesis P(k)\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{k})P(k) implies P(k+1)\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{k}+1)P(k+1))

Weak Induction, Strong Induction​

The difference between weak induction and strong indcution only appears in induction hypothesis. In weak induction, we only assume that particular statement holds at kkk-th step, while in strong induction, we assume that the particular statment holds at all the steps from the base case to kkk-th step.
