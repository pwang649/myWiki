Lagrangian Duality

The Lagrangian​

We consider an optimization problem in the standard form:

minimize⁡f0(x) subject to fi(x)≤0,i=1,…,mhi(x)=0,i=1,…,p\begin{array}{ll} \operatorname{minimize} & f_0(x) \\ \text { subject to } & f_i(x) \leq 0, \quad i=1, \ldots, m \\ & h_i(x)=0, \quad i=1, \ldots, p \end{array}minimize subject to ​f0​(x)fi​(x)≤0,i=1,…,mhi​(x)=0,i=1,…,p​

with variable x∈Rnx \in \mathbf{R}^nx∈Rn. We assume its domain D=⋂i=0mdom⁡fi∩⋂i=1pdom⁡hi\mathcal{D}=\bigcap_{i=0}^m \operatorname{dom} f_i \cap \bigcap_{i=1}^p \operatorname{dom} h_iD=⋂i=0m​domfi​∩⋂i=1p​domhi​ is nonempty, and denote the optimal value by p⋆p^{\star}p⋆. We do not assume the problem is convex.

The basic idea in Lagrangian duality is to take the constraints into account by augmenting the objective function with a weighted sum of the constraint functions. We define the Lagrangian L:Rn×Rm×Rp→RL: \mathbf{R}^n \times \mathbf{R}^m \times \mathbf{R}^p \rightarrow \mathbf{R}L:Rn×Rm×Rp→R as

L(x,λ,ν)=f0(x)+∑i=1mλifi(x)+∑i=1pνihi(x)L(x, \lambda, \nu)=f_0(x)+\sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i f_i(x)+\sum_{i=1}^p \nu_i h_i(x)L(x,λ,ν)=f0​(x)+i=1∑m​λi​fi​(x)+i=1∑p​νi​hi​(x)

with dom⁡L=D×Rm×Rp\operatorname{dom} L=\mathcal{D} \times \mathbf{R}^m \times \mathbf{R}^pdomL=D×Rm×Rp. We refer to λi\lambda_iλi​ as the Lagrange multiplier associated with the iii th inequality constraint fi(x)≤0f_i(x) \leq 0fi​(x)≤0; similarly we refer to νi\nu_iνi​ as the Lagrange multiplier associated with the iii th equality constraint hi(x)=0h_i(x)=0hi​(x)=0. The vectors λ\lambdaλ and ν\nuν are called the dual variables or Lagrange multiplier vectors.

Lower bounds on optimal value​

The dual function yields lower bounds on the optimal value p⋆p^{\star}p⋆ of the problem: For any λ⪰0\lambda \succeq 0λ⪰0 and any ν\nuν we have

g(λ,ν)≤p⋆.g(\lambda, \nu) \leq p^{\star} .g(λ,ν)≤p⋆.

The Lagrange Dual Problem​

For each pair (λ,ν)(\lambda, \nu)(λ,ν) with λ⪰0\lambda \succeq 0λ⪰0, the Lagrange dual function gives us a lower bound on the optimal value p⋆p^{\star}p⋆ of the optimization problem. Thus we have a lower bound that depends on some parameters λ,ν\lambda, \nuλ,ν. A natural question is: What is the best lower bound that can be obtained from the Lagrange dual function? This leads to the optimization problem

 maximize g(λ,ν) subject to λ⪰0.\begin{array}{ll} \text { maximize } & g(\lambda, \nu) \\ \text { subject to } & \lambda \succeq 0 . \end{array} maximize  subject to ​g(λ,ν)λ⪰0.​

This problem is called the Lagrange dual problem. In this context the original problem is sometimes called the primal problem. The term dual feasible, to describe a pair (λ,ν)(\lambda, \nu)(λ,ν) with λ⪰0\lambda \succeq 0λ⪰0 and g(λ,ν)>−∞g(\lambda, \nu)>-\inftyg(λ,ν)>−∞, now makes sense. It means, as the name implies, that (λ,ν)(\lambda, \nu)(λ,ν) is feasible for the dual problem. We refer to (λ⋆,ν⋆)\left(\lambda^{\star}, \nu^{\star}\right)(λ⋆,ν⋆) as dual optimal or optimal Lagrange multipliers if they are optimal for the dual problem.

The Lagrange dual problem is a convex optimization problem, since the objective to be maximized is concave and the constraint is convex. This is the case whether or not the primal problem is convex.
