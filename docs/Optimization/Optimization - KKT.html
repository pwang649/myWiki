Karush-Kuhn-Tucker Condition​

We consider the following problem:

 minimize f(x) subject to h(x)=0,g(x)≤0,\begin{aligned} \text { minimize } & f(\boldsymbol{x}) \\ \text { subject to } & \boldsymbol{h}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mathbf{0}, \\ & \boldsymbol{g}(\boldsymbol{x}) \leq \mathbf{0}, \end{aligned} minimize  subject to ​f(x)h(x)=0,g(x)≤0,​

where f:Rn→R,h:Rn→Rm,m≤nf: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, \boldsymbol{h}: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m, m \leq nf:Rn→R,h:Rn→Rm,m≤n, and g:Rn→Rp\boldsymbol{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^pg:Rn→Rp. For the general problem above, we adopt the following definitions.

Definition​

An inequality constraint gj(x)≤0g_j(\boldsymbol{x}) \leq 0gj​(x)≤0 is said to be active at x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ if gj(x∗)=0g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=0gj​(x∗)=0. It is inactive at x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ if gj(x∗)<0g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)<0gj​(x∗)<0.

By convention, we consider an equality constraint hi(x)=0h_i(\boldsymbol{x})=0hi​(x)=0 to be always active.

Definition​

Let x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ satisfy h(x∗)=0,g(x∗)≤0\boldsymbol{h}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{g}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \leq \mathbf{0}h(x∗)=0,g(x∗)≤0, and let J(x∗)J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)J(x∗) be the index set of active inequality constraints:

J(x∗)≜{j:gj(x∗)=0}J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \triangleq\left\{j: g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=0\right\}J(x∗)≜{j:gj​(x∗)=0}

Then, we say that x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ is a regular point if the vectors

∇hi(x∗),∇gj(x∗),1≤i≤m,j∈J(x∗)\nabla h_i\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), \quad \nabla g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), \quad 1 \leq i \leq m, \quad j \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)∇hi​(x∗),∇gj​(x∗),1≤i≤m,j∈J(x∗)

are linearly independent. We now prove a first-order necessary condition for a point to be a local minimizer. We call this condition the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)(K K T)(KKT) condition. In the literature, this condition is sometimes also called the Kuhn-Tucker condition.

Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) Theorem​

Let f, h,g∈\boldsymbol{h}, g \inh,g∈ C1\mathcal{C}^1C1. Let x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ be a regular point and a local minimizer for the problem of minimizing fff subject to h(x)=0,g(x)≤0\boldsymbol{h}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{g}(\boldsymbol{x}) \leq \mathbf{0}h(x)=0,g(x)≤0. Then, there exist λ∗∈Rm\boldsymbol{\lambda}^* \in \mathbb{R}^mλ∗∈Rm and μ∗∈Rp\boldsymbol{\mu}^* \in \mathbb{R}^pμ∗∈Rp such that:

  1. μ∗≥0\boldsymbol{\mu}^* \geq \mathbf{0}μ∗≥0
  2. Df(x∗)+λ∗⊤Dh(x∗)+μ∗⊤Dg(x∗)=0⊤D f\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\boldsymbol{\lambda}^{* \top} D \boldsymbol{h}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\boldsymbol{\mu}^{* \top} D \boldsymbol{g}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=\mathbf{0}^{\top}Df(x∗)+λ∗⊤Dh(x∗)+μ∗⊤Dg(x∗)=0⊤.
  3. μ∗⊤g(x∗)=0\boldsymbol{\mu}^{* \top} \boldsymbol{g}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=0μ∗⊤g(x∗)=0. In this theorem, we refer to λ∗\boldsymbol{\lambda}^*λ∗ as the Lagrange multiplier vector and μ∗\boldsymbol{\mu}^*μ∗ as the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) multiplier vector. We refer to their components as Lagrange multipliers and Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) multipliers, respectively.

Before proving this theorem, let us first discuss its meaning. Observe that μj∗≥0\mu_j^* \geq 0μj∗​≥0 (by condition 1 ) and gj(x∗)≤0g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \leq 0gj​(x∗)≤0. Therefore, the condition

μ∗⊤g(x∗)=μ1∗g1(x∗)+⋯+μp∗gp(x∗)=0\boldsymbol{\mu}^{* \top} \boldsymbol{g}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=\mu_1^* g_1\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\cdots+\mu_p^* g_p\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=0μ∗⊤g(x∗)=μ1∗​g1​(x∗)+⋯+μp∗​gp​(x∗)=0

implies that if gj(x∗)<0g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)<0gj​(x∗)<0, then μj∗=0\mu_j^*=0μj∗​=0; that is, for all j∉J(x∗)j \notin J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)j∈/J(x∗), we have μj∗=0\mu_j^*=0μj∗​=0. In other words, the KKT multipliers μj∗\mu_j^*μj∗​ corresponding to inactive constraints are zero. The other KKT multipliers, μi∗,i∈J(x∗)\mu_i^*, i \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)μi∗​,i∈J(x∗), are nonnegative; they may or may not be equal to zero.

Proof​

Let x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ be a regular local minimizer of fff on the set {x:h(x)=0,g(x)≤0}\{\boldsymbol{x}: \boldsymbol{h}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{g}(\boldsymbol{x}) \leq \mathbf{0}\}{x:h(x)=0,g(x)≤0}. Then, x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗ is also a regular local minimizer of fff on the set {x:h(x)=0,gj(x)=0,j∈J(x∗)}\left\{\boldsymbol{x}: \boldsymbol{h}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mathbf{0}, g_j(\boldsymbol{x})=0, j \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)\right\}{x:h(x)=0,gj​(x)=0,j∈J(x∗)} (see Exercise 21.16). Note that the latter constraint set involves only equality constraints. Therefore, from Lagrange's theorem, it follows that there exist vectors λ∗∈Rm\boldsymbol{\lambda}^* \in \mathbb{R}^mλ∗∈Rm and μ∗∈Rp\boldsymbol{\mu}^* \in \mathbb{R}^pμ∗∈Rp such that

Df(x∗)+λ∗⊤Dh(x∗)+μ∗⊤Dg(x∗)=0⊤,D f\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\boldsymbol{\lambda}^{* \top} D \boldsymbol{h}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\boldsymbol{\mu}^{* \top} D \boldsymbol{g}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=\mathbf{0}^{\top},Df(x∗)+λ∗⊤Dh(x∗)+μ∗⊤Dg(x∗)=0⊤,

where for all j∉J(x∗)j \notin J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)j∈/J(x∗), we have μj∗=0\mu_j^*=0μj∗​=0. To complete the proof it remains to show that for all j∈J(x∗)j \in J\left(x^*\right)j∈J(x∗), we have μj∗≥0\mu_j^* \geq 0μj∗​≥0 (and hence for all j=1,…,pj=1, \ldots, pj=1,…,p, we have μj∗≥0\mu_j^* \geq 0μj∗​≥0, i.e., μ∗≥0\mu^* \geq \mathbf{0}μ∗≥0 ). We use a proof by contradiction. So suppose that there exists j∈J(x∗)j \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)j∈J(x∗) such that μj∗<0\mu_j^*<0μj∗​<0. Let S^\hat{S}S^ and T^(x∗)\hat{T}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)T^(x∗) be the surface and tangent space defined by all other active constraints at x∗x^*x∗. Specifically,

S^={x:h(x)=0,gi(x)=0,i∈J(x∗),i≠j}\hat{S}=\left\{\boldsymbol{x}: \boldsymbol{h}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mathbf{0}, g_i(\boldsymbol{x})=0, i \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), i \neq j\right\}S^={x:h(x)=0,gi​(x)=0,i∈J(x∗),i=j}

and

T^(x∗)={y:Dh(x∗)y=0,Dgi(x∗)y=0,i∈J(x∗),i≠j}.\hat{T}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=\left\{\boldsymbol{y}: D \boldsymbol{h}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{0}, D g_i\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y}=0, i \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), i \neq j\right\} .T^(x∗)={y:Dh(x∗)y=0,Dgi​(x∗)y=0,i∈J(x∗),i=j}.

We claim that by the regularity of x∗\boldsymbol{x}^*x∗, there exists y∈T^(x∗)\boldsymbol{y} \in \hat{T}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)y∈T^(x∗) such that

Dgj(x∗)y≠0.D g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y} \neq 0 .Dgj​(x∗)y=0.

To see this, suppose that for all y∈T^(x∗),∇gj(x∗)⊤y=Dgj(x∗)y=0\boldsymbol{y} \in \hat{T}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), \nabla g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)^{\top} \boldsymbol{y}=D g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y}=0y∈T^(x∗),∇gj​(x∗)⊤y=Dgj​(x∗)y=0 This implies that ∇gj(x∗)∈T^(x∗)⊥\nabla g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \in \hat{T}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)^{\perp}∇gj​(x∗)∈T^(x∗)⊥. By Lemma 20.1, this, in turn, implies that

∇gj(x∗)∈span⁡[∇hk(x∗),k=1,…,m,∇gi(x∗),i∈J(x∗),i≠j]\nabla g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \in \operatorname{span}\left[\nabla h_k\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), k=1, \ldots, m, \nabla g_i\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), i \in J\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right), i \neq j\right]∇gj​(x∗)∈span[∇hk​(x∗),k=1,…,m,∇gi​(x∗),i∈J(x∗),i=j]

Df(x∗)+λ∗⊤Dh(x∗)+μj∗Dgj(x∗)+∑i≠jμi∗Dgi(x∗)=0⊤.D f\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\boldsymbol{\lambda}^{* \top} D \boldsymbol{h}\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\mu_j^* D g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)+\sum_{i \neq j} \mu_i^* D g_i\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right)=\mathbf{0}^{\top} .Df(x∗)+λ∗⊤Dh(x∗)+μj∗​Dgj​(x∗)+i=j∑​μi∗​Dgi​(x∗)=0⊤.

Df(x∗)y=−μj∗Dgj(x∗)y.D f\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y}=-\mu_j^* D g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y} .Df(x∗)y=−μj∗​Dgj​(x∗)y.

Because Dgj(x∗)y<0D g_j\left(\boldsymbol{x}^*\right) \boldsymbol{y}<0Dgj​(x∗)y<0 and we have assumed that μj∗<0\mu_j^*<0μj∗​<0, we have
