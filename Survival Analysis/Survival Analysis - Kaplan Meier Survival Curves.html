This layout is the basis upon which Kaplan-Meier survival curves are derived. The first column represents ordered survival times from smallest to largest. The second column represents frequency counts for failures at each distinct failure time. The third one represents frequency counts of those persons censored in the time interval from failure time t(f)t_{(f)}t(f)​ up to but not including the next failure time t(f+1)t_{(f+1)}t(f+1)​. The last column gives the risk set, which denotes the collection of individuals who have survived at least to time t(f)t_{(f)}t(f)​.

Each table begins with a survival time of zero, even though no subject actually failed at the start of follow-up. The reason for the zero is to allow for the possibility that some subjects might have been censored before the earliest failure time.

We also have each table contain a column denoted as nfn_fnf​ that gives the number of subjects in the risk set at the start of the interval. nfn_fnf​ counts subjects at risk for failing instantaneously prior to time t(f)t_{(f)}t(f)​.

The probability of surviving past the first ordered failure time of 111 week is given by 19/2119/2119/21 or 0.900.900.90 because 222 people failed at 111 week, so that 191919 people from the original 212121 remain as survivors past 111 week. Recall that no subject in group 2 was censored, so the qqq column for group 2 consists entirely of zeros. If some of the q's had been nonzero, an alternative formula for computing survival probabilities would be needed. This alternative formula is called the Kaplan-Meier (KM) approach and can be illustrated using the group 2 data even though all values of qqq are zero.

For instance, an alternative way to calculate the survival probability of exceeding 444 weeks for the group 2 data can be written using the KM formula shown here. This formula involves the product of conditional probability terms. That is, each term in the product is the probability of exceeding a specific ordered failure time t(f)t_{(f)}t(f)​ given that a subject survives up to that failure time. We have:

S^(4)=1⋅1921⋅1719⋅1617⋅1416=1421=0.67\hat{S}(4)=1\cdot\frac{19}{21}\cdot\frac{17}{19}\cdot\frac{16}{17}\cdot\frac{14}{16}=\frac{14}{21}=0.67S^(4)=1⋅2119​⋅1917​⋅1716​⋅1614​=2114​=0.67

Thus, in the KM formula for survival past 444 weeks, the term 19/2119/2119/21 gives the probability of surviving past the first ordered failure time, 111 week, given survival up to the first week. Note that all 212121 persons in group 2 survived up to 111 week, but that 222 failed at 111 week, leaving 191919 persons surviving past 111 week.

Similarly, the term 16/1716/1716/17 gives the probability of surviving past the third ordered failure time at week 333, given survival up to week 333. There were 171717 persons who survived up to week 333 and 111 of these then failed, leaving 161616 survivors past week 333. Note that the 171717 persons in the denominator represents the number in the risk set at week 333.

Notice that the product terms in the KM formula for surviving past 444 weeks stop at the 4th week with the component 14/1614/1614/16. Similarly, the KM formula for surviving past 888 weeks stops at the eighth week:

S^(8)=1⋅1921⋅1719⋅1617⋅1416⋅1214⋅812=821=0.38\hat{S}(8)=1\cdot\frac{19}{21}\cdot\frac{17}{19}\cdot\frac{16}{17}\cdot\frac{14}{16}\cdot\frac{12}{14}\cdot\frac{8}{12}=\frac{8}{21}=0.38S^(8)=1⋅2119​⋅1917​⋅1716​⋅1614​⋅1412​⋅128​=218​=0.38

Generally speaking, KM formula for a survival probability is limited to product terms up to the survival week being specified. Thus, KM formula is often called "product-limit" formula.
